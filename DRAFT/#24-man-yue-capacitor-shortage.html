One Stock, Two Stories: Man Yue Technology and the +580% Month vs -20% Year

The global capacitor shortage is real. The only finished-capacitor pure-play on HKEX is real. The 6.8x monthly rally on -20% profit decline is also real.

Capacitors AI Infrastructure Supply Chain HKEX Microcap

Inside every AI server sits roughly 28,000 multilayer ceramic capacitors, or MLCCs. A standard server needs about 2,200. That 27-fold jump in capacitor intensity per machine has driven lead times from 8 weeks to over 20 weeks since February 2026. High-capacity MLCC prices are up 15 to 35%. Scarce models are doubling on spot markets. Goldman Sachs calls this the largest and longest MLCC upcycle on record. The shortage is structural, and Japanese incumbents are tapped at 95% capacity with new fabs not coming online until 2027. That is the real story. The other story is this: Man Yue Technology (0894.HK), the only finished-capacitor pure-play trading on HKEX, rallied 6.8 times in a single month ending June 4 while its FY2025 profit attributable to shareholders fell 20.1%. The theme is real. The disconnect between the narrative and the numbers is also real.

What Man Yue Technology does

Man Yue Technology Holdings Limited has manufactured aluminum electrolytic capacitors since 1979. The company is headquartered in Sheung Wan, Hong Kong. Its production bases sit in Dongguan and Wuxi, mainland China. The company sells under three brands: SAMXON, X-CON, and XLPC. It makes three core products. Aluminum electrolytic capacitors, called E-Caps, are the traditional workhorse for power management in consumer electronics. Conductive polymer aluminum solid capacitors, or Polymer Caps, are the higher-performance variant for demanding applications. Electric double-layer capacitors, or EDLC, are supercapacitors used for rapid energy storage and backup power. That is the business. Passive components for IT, telecommunications, and electrical brands.

Why the discount exists

The valuation is not the problem. Man Yue trades at roughly a 15% premium to its global aluminum-electrolytic-capacitor peers. That premium is explained by three factors. First, Hong Kong retail investors have limited direct access to pure-play capacitor manufacturers. Most production is in Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. Second, the company holds nearly HKD 300 million in net cash against a tiny market capitalization. Third, and most important, the current rally has decoupled the share price from the business fundamentals. The FY2025 annual report, filed with HKEX on April 27, 2026, tells a flat story. Revenue grew a mere 2.3% year on year. Gross margin improved slightly to 16.6% from 15.3%. But operating profit declined to HKD 59.9 million from HKD 63.4 million. Management attributed this drop to foreign exchange losses from RMB appreciation. Profit attributable to shareholders fell 20.1% to HKD 6.25 million. Earnings per share dropped to 1.31 HK cents from 1.64. This is not a growth company delivering breakout results. It is a steady-state manufacturer FX-hedged into a profit decline.

The catalyst: AI servers, structural shortage, and an empty order book

But something changed in the first half of 2026. The Chairman's Statement in that same FY2025 report pivots the narrative explicitly. The company frames its future around "artificial intelligence (AI), energy storage solutions, power grids solutions, low-altitude economy, and robotics." It describes 2025 as a "transformation from a capacitors' supplier to a comprehensive solutions provider." That is the language of a company positioning itself for the AI infrastructure buildout. The language aligns with a real, independently verified shortage. TrendForce projects a "structural shortage" in MLCCs for the second half of 2026. The research firm documents that AMD's MI450 platform saw a 632% increase in one capacitor specification. Nvidia platform requirements rose from 320 to 500 units per board. AI servers now require approximately 28,000 MLCCs with 600,000μF total capacitance, compared to 2,200 MLCCs in traditional servers. That is a 27-fold jump per machine. Lead times on large-case components have extended beyond 20 weeks. High-capacitance MLCC prices have risen 15 to 20% since February 2026. AI-specific grades have jumped 50 to 60%. Scarce models are doubling on spot markets. Goldman Sachs, quoted in the South China Morning Post, calls this the largest and longest MLCC upcycle on record. The bottleneck is supply. Japanese firms Murata and Samsung Electro-Mechanics control roughly 54% of the roughly USD 17 billion global MLCC market. They are running at above 95% capacity utilization. New production facilities will not fully ramp until 2027. Order spillover is already happening. Taiwan's Chinsan is gaining business that Japanese suppliers cannot fulfill.

That is the genuine backdrop. Man Yue is not an MLCC manufacturer. It makes aluminum electrolytic capacitors, conductive polymer capacitors, and supercapacitors. But aluminum electrolytic capacitors face a parallel squeeze. Nichicon, the second-largest global supplier, notified customers of a blanket 10 to 15% price increase. Raw material costs for aluminum foil, chemicals, and electricity are rising. More importantly, the supercapacitor angle is not fantasy. Panasonic is developing rack-mounted Capacitor Backup Units using supercapacitors to smooth the wildly oscillating power load of AI servers. Production is targeted for fiscal 2027. IEEE Spectrum has covered supercapacitors as a grid-smoothing technology for AI power spikes. Man Yue manufactures EDLC supercapacitors. The product line exists. The use case is real. What is missing is the confirmed order book.

Man Yue has not published its H1 2026 interim results. Those are due in August or September 2026. As of July 11, 2026, the company has not filed a positive profit alert disclosing an AI or datacenter-linked order book. The entire investment case rests on a narrative pivot in the Chairman's Statement and the price action that followed. The rally itself has been explosive. On a single day in May 2026, Man Yue shares jumped 34.15% to close at HKD 1.65 on volume of 27.4 million shares, nearly 30 times the average daily volume. Chinese financial media framed this explicitly as a concept-stock rally. Sina Finance reported on May 28 that the stock surged over 68% intraday on rising "supercapacitor concept" heat. On June 4, Sina reported the cumulative gain over the trailing month exceeded 6.8 times. That is a roughly 580% increase in four weeks on a microcap with declining profit. The market has priced in a transformative AI order book that has not yet been disclosed in any filing.

Valuation

The simplest way to value Man Yue is against its global peers. On a trailing price-to-earnings basis, the stock trades at roughly 13.2 times earnings. This compares to Nichicon at 12.5 times, Panasonic at 11.8 times, Rubycon at 10.9 times, and Nippon Chemi-Con at 9.7 times. The premium to peers is approximately 15%. That premium is not egregious. It can be justified by the net cash position of nearly HKD 300 million and the scarcity value of a HKEX-listed pure-play in a tight market. But valuation misses the point. The question is not whether 13.2 times earnings is fair. The question is whether those earnings themselves are about to inflect upward. The FY2025 net profit of HKD 6.25 million implies a market capitalization at the current rally price of roughly HKD 500 million to HKD 600 million. That would be a 3 to 4% earnings yield on a net-cash-positive balance sheet. The math is not broken. The risk is that the base is static. If the AI thesis is real, H1 2026 results should show margin expansion and order-book growth. If the thesis is purely narrative, the results will show another flat quarter with FX headwinds. The market has priced the first outcome. The discount exists because neither outcome has been confirmed.

Risks

Liquidity and sentiment risk. Man Yue is a microcap with thin trading. The 6.8x monthly rally was driven by retail concept-stock momentum, not institutional accumulation of a proven compounder. If H1 2026 results disappoint or simply fail to meet AI-fueled expectations, the air can come out quickly. The stock doubled from less than HKD 1.00 to over HKD 5.00 in weeks on hype. A reversion to the pre-rally price on flat fundamentals is entirely plausible.

Product mix mismatch. The core shortage is in high-capacitance MLCCs for AI servers. Man Yue does not make MLCCs. It makes aluminum electrolytic capacitors and supercapacitors. The aluminum-electrolytic market faces price pressure from Nichicon's increase, but that is a pass-through cost story, not a capacity-constraint story. The supercapacitor angle for AI power backup is real and documented by Panasonic, but it is an emerging use case. Man Yue faces competition from dedicated supercapacitor specialists and from integrated capacitor manufacturers expanding into EDLC. The product fit is plausible but not guaranteed.

Decision

The AI-driven capacitor shortage is documented, structural, and persistent through 2027. Japanese incumbents are capacity-constrained. Order spillover to second-tier suppliers is happening. Supercapacitors for AI power backup are a genuine emerging application. That is the setup. The specific bet on Man Yue Technology is this: that H1 2026 interim results, due in August or September, will show the company capturing AI-linked demand. The Chairman's Statement repositioned the company toward AI, energy storage, and robotics. The stock rallied 6.8x on that narrative. But the FY2025 results showed profit down 20%. No AI order book has been disclosed. The market has priced in a transformation that remains unproven in the filings.

There is only one finished-capacitor pure-play trading on HKEX. That is a real scarcity value. But the shortage trade is crowded in Man Yue. The stock has moved 580% in a month on a thesis that has not yet been confirmed in a single filing. The risk is not that the capacitor shortage is fake. The risk is that Man Yue is not the beneficiary, or not yet. Wait for the interim results. If the AI order book is real, it will appear in the numbers. If it does not, the stock will re-rate without it.
