China Tripled Its GDP. The Hang Seng Didn't Move.

The Hang Seng Index closed last week at roughly the same level it did 16 years ago. Over that same period, China's economy tripled, Shanghai's tech scene led the world, and infrastructure rivals the West. The riddle resolves when you separate economic growth from shareholder returns, and understand what changed in Beijing's treatment of capital.

Key Takeaway

The Hang Seng's 16-year flat return is not irrational. It correctly prices a market where growth accrues to the state, rule-changing is retroactive, the old business model (property-fueled credit) is broken, and institutional capital has better options elsewhere. The 8-9x valuation discount to the S&P 500 is compensation for structural risk, not a bargain.

The numbers are stark. The Hang Seng in December 2010 closed at around 23,000. Today it is at 23,310. The S&P 500 has gone from 1,258 to 5,450 in the same 16 years (4.3x). The Nikkei from 10,250 to 38,000 (3.7x). China's nominal GDP from $6.1 trillion to $19.4 trillion (3.2x). Zero price return on the benchmark of the world's second-largest economy while every other major market doubled or tripled.

The instinct is to assume the market is broken. The better reading is that the market is working correctly: it is pricing an economic growth story that never belonged to minority shareholders in the first place.

GDP growth ≠ shareholder profit

This is the foundational misunderstanding. When economists celebrate China's 3x expansion, they are measuring the national cake. They are not measuring who gets to eat it. China's growth model is investment-heavy, credit-fueled, and state-directed. A highways company doubles revenue by building empty capacity. A steel mill triples output by expanding state production. A bank multiplies its balance sheet by the credit multiplier. The GDP statistics light up. The distributable profits to minority equity holders tell a different story.

Return on equity (ROE) for Chinese-listed companies has lagged global peers for 16 years. Growth accrued to the state, to local governments via land sales, and to the credit system. The minority shareholder sat last in line. The Hang Seng index did not miss a growth story. It correctly priced a growth story that was not a profits story.

The index pivoted into the crackdown

For most of the 2010s, the Hang Seng was a play on the old economy. HSBC, ICBC, and BOC carried the index. Property developers like Sun Hung Kai and CK Asset were stable, slow-growth dividend payers. Then, around 2018-2021, the composition shifted. Alibaba, Meituan, Xiaomi, Tencent, and JD.com entered or gained prominence. The Hang Seng Tech Index was launched. Finally, the index was positioned to capture China's most dynamic growth engine.

The timing was catastrophic. Within months, Beijing unleashed the most aggressive corporate crackdown in modern Chinese history. Ant Group's IPO halted. Alibaba faced antitrust fines. Tencent's gaming revenue was capped. Didi, which had just gone public on the NYSE, was forced to delist. The entire for-profit K-12 education sector was wiped out. The market's reading was not about specific company pain. It was: the state can retroactively rewrite the rules. Your property rights as a shareholder are contingent on whether Beijing approves. Alibaba fell from $230 to $65-85. Tencent, despite owning world-class franchises comparable to Microsoft, trades at a fraction of the US peer multiple.

The old model broke. Capital fled.

Property and construction were the backbone of China's two-decade growth expansion. "Three red lines" limits on developer debt (2020) triggered defaults: Evergrande, Country Garden, Sunac. Land sales collapsed. Local governments lost revenue. Household wealth eroded.

At the same time, Hong Kong became the main valve for global money exiting China. International investors downgraded China from "core EM allocation" to "tactical" or "none." Flows rotated to India, Taiwan, ASEAN, and "EM ex-China" strategies. The selling was structural, not cyclical. Added to this: the 2019 pro-democracy protests, the 2020 National Security Law, and questions about Hong Kong's autonomy created a geopolitical risk premium that did not exist in 2010.

Why developed markets soared, and China did not

The S&P 500's 4.3x return came from globally dominant tech platforms (Apple, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Alphabet) that captured AI and digitalization upside, paired with predictable regulation, strong investor protections, and massive shareholder buybacks. The Nikkei's 3.7x came from Abenomics, corporate governance reforms, and policy explicitly designed to raise return on equity and lift price-to-book above 1. Policy was directionally pro-shareholder.

In China, policy from 2020 onwards moved explicitly away from shareholder primacy. State and social priorities took precedence, even at the cost of valuation. Beijing said so explicitly. This was not a secret.

The valuation discount is justified

The Hang Seng today trades at 8-9x forward earnings. The S&P 500 trades at 20-22x. This is not irrational exuberance on one side and stupidity on the other. The multiple reflects risk. Risk that the state can retroactively change the rules. Risk that the growth model is broken. Risk that policy prioritizes other objectives over shareholder returns. Risk that geopolitics continues to weigh on flows. Risk that the currency moves against international holders.

Whether that risk is fairly priced today is a separate question and a trader's call. The 16-year flat return is not a mystery. The market is not broken. It is doing exactly what it should: pricing growth that accrues to the state, and risk that accrues to shareholders.

What would revalue China equities

Three conditions would need to align for institutional capital to return to Hong Kong equities. None of them are in place yet, but naming them clarifies what investors are actually waiting for.

First: Policy reversal on shareholder primacy. The state would need to signal that returning cash to minority shareholders — via dividends, buybacks, or genuine ROE improvements — is a priority, not a side effect. This does not require the West's shareholder-first model. Japan and South Korea both deliver strong returns to shareholders within a state-directed framework. China would need to simply choose to do so. A concrete signal: allowing tech and private companies to run sustained buyback programs without regulatory interference, or opening the door to higher dividend payouts. Beijing's statements since 2023 have occasionally warmed to private enterprise, but actions — not words — are what unlock capital flows. A single major tech company handed buyback authority and allowed to execute it without sudden policy reversal would reset expectations overnight.

Second: A durable fix to the property sector. The old growth model will not return. But a clear plan for the new one would reduce the tail risk that depresses multiples. That plan does not exist yet. Evergrande, Country Garden, and Sunac remain in limbo. Local governments still face revenue pressure from collapsed land sales. Household confidence in property as a store of wealth has not recovered. International investors need to see either a credible real-estate stabilization program (write-downs, consolidation, recapitalization) or an alternative growth driver that does not depend on credit-fueled expansion. Neither is evident as of mid-2026. Stability here would unlock the "risk-off" premium that today anchors Hang Seng valuations below historical norms.

Third: Geopolitical thaw or acceptance of permanent discount. Investors priced in a US-China escalation as a baseline scenario. A visible de-escalation would remove a layer of risk premium. Absent that, the market will accept a persistent discount — not as a bargain, but as the normal price for Hong Kong equities in a contested geopolitical environment. The US-China relationship has not meaningfully improved since 2020. If it remains frozen at current temperature, capital will not return out of hope — it will simply price Hong Kong as a "geopolitical discount" asset class, comparable to how emerging-market currencies trade at permanent discounts to the dollar. That outcome is stable, not catastrophic. It just means 8-9x is the new normal, not a temporary bottom waiting to be bought.

The timeline for any of these three to materialize is unclear. Policy shifts can happen rapidly (Beijing signaled its tech crackdown in weeks, not years). But a genuine reversal on shareholder primacy would require admitting that the 2020-2023 strategy depressed valuations and growth. That admission may not come for years, if ever. International investors are patient. They will wait for proof. They will not return on promises.
