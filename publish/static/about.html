About

I read the Hong Kong filings most people skip. When the price and the math disagree, I write down why.

Who I am

I have been reading annual reports for years. Hong Kong, mostly. At some point reading them stopped being enough, so I started writing the situations down. Trading852 is where they live: a public record of the companies I found cheap enough, and clear enough, to work through in full.

I am not a licensed analyst. I do not manage anyone's money. I have nothing to sell you and no newsletter to upsell. What I have is time, a methodical reading habit, and a preference for what a company owns over what the market feels about it.

What I am trying to do

One thing, repeated. Find a company where the market's story and the filing's arithmetic point in opposite directions. Measure the gap. Name the event that would close it. Then publish the work before it closes.

A cheap company with no mechanism to re-rate is a value trap. A cheap company with a credible trigger is a situation worth writing about. I am only interested in the second kind.

Every write-up carries a verdict: conviction, monitor, or avoid. The verdict is whatever the work returns, not what I went in hoping for. All of it goes on the Scorecard, right or wrong, where the record stays public.

Why Hong Kong

Because it is one of the few markets where doing the work still pays.

Coverage on mid-cap Hong Kong conglomerates is thin. The structures are tangled enough that most institutions do not bother. And since 2021 the market has been in the news for the wrong reasons, so quality businesses get priced as if the discount is permanent.

Many of them generate real cash, pay dividends, sit on net cash, and are run by insiders who own enough to care. The headline about Hong Kong and the number in the filing are often two different things. I look for the cases where the gap is measurable and something specific can close it.

How the work happens

It starts with the chart, not the story. The chart shows me where the price has been and what the market already believes. Then I go to the filing, and I go to it before I read a single sell-side summary. A view built from someone else's summary is just their view wearing my name.

The core method is sum-of-parts. Break the company into its divisions, value each on observable peer multiples, add the balance sheet, subtract the debt. That gives a range for what the thing is worth. Then I set that range against the price.

If the gap is large, one question is left: what closes it, and on what timeline. A buyback, a privatisation offer, an asset sale, a dividend, an earnings inflection. When a case holds up under that question, I write it here in full: the numbers, the valuation, the bull and the bear, and the one trigger I am watching. Nothing sits behind a paywall.

What this is not

This is not advice. I am not telling you what to buy or sell, or when. I publish because writing forces me to think straight, and because I would rather read research that is honest about its assumptions than research that pretends it has none.

Every write-up is a point in time. Markets move, facts change, and a margin of safety that looked wide in April can look thin by August. Read the filings yourself. Build your own model. Decide for yourself. And read the disclaimer before you act on anything here.
